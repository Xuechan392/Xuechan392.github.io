Personal History

The Story Between
Me and Linguistics

From a secondhand cassette player in Guizhou to a phonology lab at UVA — this is how a fear of my own voice became the thing I study.

I
Guizhou · Childhood
The Hierarchy of Accents

I grew up in Guiyang, the capital of Guizhou Province — a place most people outside of China know little about, except perhaps that it is one of the country's poorest provinces. Guizhou is also one of its most linguistically diverse: home to over 33 ethnic minorities and at least 12 distinct minority languages, it is a place where the sounds of human language are unusually varied.

I didn't know any of that growing up. What I knew was a simpler, crueler geography: accents had a hierarchy. People from the countryside were mocked by people from county towns; county town accents were mocked by city speakers; Guizhou accents in general were dismissed by people from other provinces as sounding like "tǔ dòu wèi" — literally, "tasting of potatoes." Raw. Rural. Not quite right.

I internalized this hierarchy early. I became intensely aware of how I sounded — and deeply anxious about it. I wanted to speak in a way that nobody could mock.

II
Elementary School · A Turning Point
The Cassette Player

My solution, at around age ten, was a secondhand cassette player and a set of New Concept English tapes. I would sit in our small apartment, rewind the tape, listen to a sentence, and repeat it — trying to match the speaker sound by sound, syllable by syllable. Then rewind. Repeat. Again.

What I was actually doing, though I had no name for it then, was training myself to hear fine phonetic distinctions. I was developing an ear for the difference between what a sound was and what it should be. I was, without knowing it, doing informal acoustic phonetics on myself.

In sixth grade, this obsession led me somewhere unexpected: I represented Guizhou in the national "Hope Star" English speech competition and placed seventh in the country. For a kid from one of China's poorest provinces, in a competition dominated by students from Beijing and Shanghai, this felt impossible.

It also felt like a signal — though I couldn't have articulated it at the time — that paying close attention to sound was something I was genuinely good at.

III
BLCU · 2017–2021
The Syntax Tree Moment

I studied Teaching Chinese as a Second Language at Beijing Language and Culture University — not because it was my first choice, but because China's universities didn't yet offer standalone linguistics degrees. I wanted to work with language. I just hadn't found the right frame for it yet.

That frame arrived in my third year, unexpectedly, when I wandered into a syntax course taught by Professor Sī Fùzhēn. She drew a sentence on the board and began to break it into a tree.

I had never seen anything like it. Here was language — something I had always felt but never been able to hold still — rendered as a structure. A system with rules that could be drawn, tested, argued over. I remember thinking: this is what I've been looking for.

I decided to apply for a linguistics master's program. I studied hard. My subject score ranked first among all applicants. Then the politics exam results came back: I had failed by two points.

I was devastated. It is hard to describe how it feels to watch a door you worked toward close on you because of a subject that has nothing to do with what you love. But it also, eventually, pushed me toward a different path — one that would take me much further than I had imagined.

IV
University of Michigan · 2021–2022
Learning to Exist in English

I arrived in Ann Arbor in the fall of 2021 for an M.Ed. in Educational Studies — the closest path available to me at the time. And I promptly fell apart.

I could not order food at a restaurant. I could not speak in class. I was afraid to go to the pharmacy when I was sick. For more than six months, I barely left my apartment except to attend lectures — where I sat in silence, convinced that the moment I opened my mouth, the gap between who I sounded like and who I was trying to be would be painfully obvious to everyone.

The anxiety I had developed as a child — about the Guizhou accent, about not sounding right — had followed me across the Pacific and attached itself to English.

What changed things was a research assistantship. I emailed Professor San Duanmu on a long shot, asking if he needed help. He said yes. We worked on verb-object length restrictions in Mandarin — a corpus study, counting characters and running statistics. It was small work. But it put me in a room with someone who talked about language the way I had always hoped someone would.

"No language is more 'advanced' than another. A linguist would never dismiss a variety because of where it comes from. Even native speakers make speech errors — and those errors aren't failures. They're data."

— Prof. San Duanmu, University of Michigan

These words did something to me that I'm still untangling. They weren't just a statement about linguistics. They were a statement about me — about the Guiyang accent I had spent my life trying to sand down. What if the "potato taste" wasn't a defect? What if it was a data point?

V
Williams College & UVA · 2024–present
From Teaching to Research — Again

For several years, I put research aside and became a teacher — at Middlebury, at Williams College as a Language Fellow, through PiPa Magazine. I love teaching. I'm good at it. But sitting with a textbook in front of a class, I kept stopping at the same question: why does this sentence work this way? What is happening, structurally, when a learner's brain produces that error?

Teaching was not answering those questions. It was raising them, repeatedly, and then moving on.

So I enrolled in a third master's program — this time in linguistics, at UVA. I know that three master's degrees is an unusual path. The simplest way I can explain it: I needed to be certain. Certain that I actually wanted to do research, not just think about it. Certain that I could.

In the fall of 2025, in a phonology course, I chose to study the variety I grew up speaking. For the first time in my academic life, my own voice was the object of study rather than an obstacle to overcome.

Guiyang Mandarin turned out to be more interesting than I had expected. The tone sandhi patterns are unusual. One of the kinship terms I studied — 婆婆 — surfaces with a different sandhi pattern depending on whether it means "paternal grandmother" or "mother-in-law." The same form. The same structure. But the phonology tracks the semantics.

I don't fully understand it yet. That's why I'm going back.

VI
Coda
What I Want to Do With All of This

I am a first-generation college student. The first person in my family to study abroad. My parents have never been to a university. When they visited Beijing once, people could hear where they were from the moment they spoke — and they were treated accordingly.

I want to be a researcher and a teacher. But I also want to be the kind of person who can look a student in the eye and tell them, with real evidence: your accent is not a flaw in your reasoning. Your dialect is not a sign of limited intelligence. The variety you grew up with is linguistically as interesting as any other — and quite possibly more interesting, because fewer people have bothered to look at it carefully.

Guizhou has 33 ethnic minorities. It has a dozen languages that interact with Mandarin in ways we don't yet understand. It has a Mandarin variety with tone sandhi patterns that appear sensitive to semantics in ways that most theories haven't accounted for.

I grew up there. I know what it sounds like from the inside. That's not a limitation. That's a research agenda.
