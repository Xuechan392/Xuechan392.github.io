Teaching

Teaching Experience

Five years of Chinese language instruction across university, immersion, and online contexts. Teaching has sharpened the questions I now pursue as a researcher — particularly around phonological acquisition and how learners internalize tonal contrasts.

Approach
Teaching Philosophy

My years in the classroom taught me something I couldn't have learned any other way: the moment a learner produces an unexpected form is not a failure to be corrected and moved past. It is a window into how language works.

That conviction now shapes both how I teach and what I research. In the classroom, I try to build environments where students feel safe producing imperfect output — because imperfect output is where learning actually happens. In my research, I pursue the same question from the other direction: what is the underlying system that generates those forms?

I have taught Chinese at levels from beginner to advanced, in immersion and non-immersion settings, to heritage learners, L2 learners, and children. Across all of those contexts, the thing that works best is honest attention to how language is actually structured.

"The student's error is not noise. It is data — and sometimes the most interesting data in the room."
